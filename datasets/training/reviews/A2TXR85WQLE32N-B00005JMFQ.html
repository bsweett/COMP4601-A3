It's hard not to like "Love Actually", 2004's whirlwind romantic comedy set around a series of couples in the weeks before Christmas in London. Everyone's romantic situation is under stress; how those situations are worked out is the engaging, funny theme of this holiday movie.

Some subplots are fairly obvious: a single Prime Minister (Hugh Grant) who falls for his attractive young caterer; a widower (Liam Neeson) helping his young stepson through his first big crush; a writer (Colin Firth) fleeing a failed relationship who falls for his Portuguese maid; and a middle-aged wife (Emma Thompson) who suspects her husband is cheating. Other subplots are more subtle: a young man's affection for his best friend's new bride; two porn actors exploring a real relationship; an office worker in love with a co-worker; an aging rocker facing a lonely Christmas; and a sex-crazed youth seeking an American female.

Managing this many storylines becomes a point of dramatic tension. Director Richard Curtis keeps the action moving, sliding us past a few gimmicks while lingering in the right places. The result is heartwarming, humorous, suprisingly honest, and sometimes absurd. The story is well-seeded with distinctly British humor and surprise cameos. "Love Actually" is very highly recommended as worthwhile romantic comedy.
