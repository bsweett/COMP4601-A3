Dark City works as a concept movie (what is the nature of reality?),but in practice it doesn't work out as well. It makes a good cinematic introduction to Buddhism,superior to the Buddhism/Taoism lite of Star Wars. Dark City even leaves the viewer with questions,making the shiny happy ending bittersweet.

Rufus Sewell,who resembles a young Ian McShane,is John Murdock,an insomniac who has been framed for murder.While he's trying to solve the mystery,he encounters the detective,Bumstead,a half-hearted performance from William Hurt, and the mad stuttering scientist Dr.Schreber,expertly played by Kiefer Sutherland. Sutherland shows his versatility as an actor;in contrast to his character on "24",he plays a maddened,bumbling scientist. Jennifer Connelly stars as Anna,John's wife. Her role is primarily to be a double agent&a damsel in distress.

In the end,John learns that the Strangers (who look like a cross between the Borg,Nosferatu&Pinhead) are trying to find out what makes humans human through their experiments,and want to be immortal because their race is dying out. Since they're all male (except for a demon child),one wonders if an alternate title could be "The Matrix Needs Women." The last battle between John&the leader of the Strangers (Ian Richardson,who was an evil British Prime Minister in a BBC series)is a cheesy metaphysical shoot 'em up.It goes on and on and on.It comes across as a staring contest crossed with a fireworks show.

However,the movie brings up intriguing ideas,especially Buddhist ones. Sutherland's character brings up reincarnation ("you have lived many lives"),that reality is an illusion,and that it's a person's responsibility to create their own world.In the last battle,he tells Sewell that he "must concentrate." Concentration is important to all forms of Buddhism.

There are also aspects of Dark City that are intriguingly open-ended:
Were the Strangers a figment of John's imagination? Was he hallucinating due to his insomnia?
Where were the people of the Dark City from before they were abducted?
Was it all John's dream?
When John reunites with his wife,is he still within the Dark City,making memories that are only an illusion?

While the movie has its cheesy aspects (the Strangers floating around,having hilarious names&menacingly intoning "Sleep"),it does bring up fascinating philosophical questions.It makes for fun viewing.
