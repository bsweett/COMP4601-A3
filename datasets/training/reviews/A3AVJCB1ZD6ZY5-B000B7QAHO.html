Many years ago, Charlie Button's grandfather worked for Willy Wonka's Choclate Factory. Their chocolates were amazing. Seemed like Wonka could invent candy other folks just dreamed of making---like ice cream that never melted even on the hottest day. So amazing, in fact, that a good many of the employees performed industrial espionage. Soon, Wonka's competitors were offering no-melt ice cream and gum that didn't lose its flavor.

Willy could have no idea that at least one or two of his competitors could steal in no time flat.

Sadly, Willy Wonka closed the door on the factory and let all his employees go. No one's been in there since, but the choclate keeps pouring out.

In an unprecedented move, Willy offers five golden tickets in his Wonka Bars. The children who win these five tickets get to visit the chocolate factory for a day--and one will come away with an unheard of prize: which Willy will not list!

Charlie badly wants to win the prize, both for himself and his grandfather. But, how does poor Charlie stand a chance if he only gets one bar of chocolate per year---on his birthday.

Meanwhile, kids are finding the Golden Tickets. First, there's a fat German kid who eats chocolate all the time. Then, there's a spoilt British girl whose industrialist father buys her hundreds of thousands of chocolate bars just so she can get the one. Then, a kid hacker from Colorado who pegs the system and knows just which bar to buy. Finally, the world's champion gum chewer and her stage mom win.

They're down to one. Charlie finds a dollar in the snow and gets the last ticket.

The next day is the tour of the chocolate factory. Wonka, the five kids and their respective parents go on an amazing tour where they discover that a tiny race of people--the oompah loompahs--are who now work at the factory creating choclates. Throughout the tour, each of the children is tested---til only one remains.

The effects in this film are pretty amazing. I will admit I wasn't certain Depp could pull the role off, but he did. He's a somewhat more disociated Wonka than Gene Wilder, but still interesting. Overall, a very enjoyable film and a sweet deal for both the young and the young at heart.
