This thought-provoking movie begins with the following narration by one of its major characters, a scientist named Dr. Daniel Poe Schreber (Kiefer Sutherland):

"First there was darkness, then came The Strangers. They were a race as time itself. They had mastered the ultimate technology: the ability to alter physical reality by will alone. They called this ability "tuning." But they were dying. Their civilization was in decline and so they abandoned their world seeking a cure for their own mortality. Their endless journey brought them to a small blue world in the farthest corner of the Galaxy-our world. Here they thought they had finally found what they had been searching for..."

The actual story then begins with John Murdoch (Rufus Sewell) awakening in a strange hotel room where he finds that he's wanted for a series of murders. But the problem is that he can't remember a thing. Pursued by the police led by Inspector Frank Bumstead (William Hurt) and haunted by The Strangers, he tries to discover the riddle of his identity. But in a city where reality is the ultimate illusion, discovering the truth proves to be a major challenge.

The viewer is kept guessing for the first two-thirds of this movie and its in the final one-third that all answers as to what exactly is going on is revealed.

A highlight of this movie is the fantastic visuals brought about by mind-boggling special effects. I thus really believed that The Strangers could alter physical reality (as mentioned in the above narration).

Many people did not like Kiefer Sutherland's acting where he gave the scientist character a breathy, almost Peter Lorre type voice. I, however, liked his performance. Also look for the fine performance by William Hurt as the police inspector.

Finally, the DVD is practically flawless in picture and sound quality. It has a host of interesting extras, including an audio commentary by Roger Ebert.

In conclusion, this is a thought-provoking movie that truly "sets a bold new standard in science-fiction." Be sure to see it, and find out why Roger Ebert named this movie in 1998 as best movie of the year!!

(1998; 95 min; double-sided disc; widescreen/full screen; 16 scenes)
